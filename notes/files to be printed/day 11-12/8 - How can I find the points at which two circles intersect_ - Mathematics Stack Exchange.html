This can be done without any trigonometry at all. Let the equations of the circles be

(x−x1)2+(y−y1)2=r21,(1)(1)(x−x1)2+(y−y1)2=r12,
(x−x2)2+(y−y2)2=r22.(2)(2)(x−x2)2+(y−y2)2=r22.
By subtracting (2)(2) from (1)(1) and then expanding, we in fact obtain a linear equation for xx and yy; after a little rearranging it becomes
−2x(x1−x2)−2y(y1−y2)=(r21−r22)−(x21−x22)−(y21−y22).−2x(x1−x2)−2y(y1−y2)=(r12−r22)−(x12−x22)−(y12−y22).
(If the circles intersect, this is the equation of the line that passes through the intersection points.) This equation can be solved for one of xx or yy; let's suppose y1−y2≠0y1−y2≠0 so that we can solve for yy:
y=−x1−x2y1−y2x+….(3)(3)y=−x1−x2y1−y2x+….
Substituting this expression for yy into (1)(1) or (2)(2) gives a quadratic equation in only xx. Then the xx-coordinates of the intersection points are the solutions to this; the yy-coordinates can be obtained by plugging the xx-coordinates into (3)(3).

Easy solution is to consider another plane such that the centers are along an axis.

Given the points (x1,y1)(x1,y1) and (x2,y2)(x2,y2). We focus on the center point of both circles given by

(x1+x22,y1+y22).(x1+x22,y1+y22).

The distance between the centers of the circles is given by

R=(x2−x1)2+(y2−y1)2−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−√.R=(x2−x1)2+(y2−y1)2.

We can consider the following orthogonal vectors

a⃗ =(x2−x1R,y2−y1R),b⃗ =(y2−y1R,−x2−x1R).a→=(x2−x1R,y2−y1R),b→=(y2−y1R,−x2−x1R).

In the (a⃗ ,b⃗ )(a→,b→) plane we get the equations

(a+R/2)2+b2=r21,(a−R/2)2+b2=r22.(a+R/2)2+b2=r12,(a−R/2)2+b2=r22.

Whence

a=r21−r222R,b=±r21+r222−(r21−r22)24R2−R24−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−√.a=r12−r222R,b=±r12+r222−(r12−r22)24R2−R24.
The intersection points are given by
(x,y)=12(x1+x2,y1+y2)+r21−r222R2(x2−x1,y2−y1)±122r21+r22R2−(r21−r22)2R4−1−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−√(y2−y1,x1−x2),(x,y)=12(x1+x2,y1+y2)+r12−r222R2(x2−x1,y2−y1)±122r12+r22R2−(r12−r22)2R4−1(y2−y1,x1−x2),
 where RR is the distance between the centers of the circles.

A nice way to look at this is to first consider the case when one point is at the origin and the other lies on the x-axis. Let the points be at (0,0)(0,0), (d,0)(d,0) and the radii be r1r1, r2r2. The two equations simplify to

x2+y2=r21x2+y2=r12
and
(x−d)2+y2=r22(x−d)2+y2=r22
Use the first to find y2y2 and substitute in the second.
(x−d)2+r21−x2=r22(x−d)2+r12−x2=r22
expand and simplify
−2xd+d2+r21=r22−2xd+d2+r12=r22
so
x=r21−r22+d22dx=r12−r22+d22d
and from Pythagorus
y=r21−x2−−−−−−√.y=r12−x2.
This part came from mathworld.

For the general position case with points (x1,y1)(x1,y1) (x2,y2)(x2,y2) let

dlh=(x1−x2)2+(y1−y2)2−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−√=r21−r22+d22d=r21−l2−−−−−−√d=(x1−x2)2+(y1−y2)2l=r12−r22+d22dh=r12−l2
Now (x2−x1d,y2−y1d)(x2−x1d,y2−y1d) (y2−y1d,−x2−x1d)(y2−y1d,−x2−x1d) are two orthogonal unit vectors and we can rotate and translate to get the general solution
xy=ld(x2−x1)±hd(y2−y1)+x1,=ld(y2−y1)∓hd(x2−x1)+y1.x=ld(x2−x1)±hd(y2−y1)+x1,y=ld(y2−y1)∓hd(x2−x1)+y1.

Let C1=(x1,y1),C2=(x2,y2)C1=(x1,y1),C2=(x2,y2) be the centers of the two circles and r1,r2r1,r2 be their radii respectively.

Their equations are

(x−x1)2+(y−y1)2=r21(x−x1)2+(y−y1)2=r12
(x−x2)2+(y−y2)2=r22(x−x2)2+(y−y2)2=r22

They intersect only iff |r1−r2|≤|C1−C2|≤|r1+r2||r1−r2|≤|C1−C2|≤|r1+r2|, where |C1−C2||C1−C2| is the distance between the two centers. If equality holds, the circles touch and there is one solution. For strict inequalities, they intersect and they have two solutions.

Just solve the system of equations. Suppose that x0x0 is a point on the first circle. Then, its parametric representation is x0=(x1+r1cosθ,y1+r1sinθ)x0=(x1+r1cos⁡θ,y1+r1sin⁡θ) for some θθ. If x0x0 also lies on the second circle, which will make it a point of intersection, it must also satisfy the equation of the second circle i.e.

(x0−x2)2+(y0−y2)2=r22(x0−x2)2+(y0−y2)2=r22
Substitute the parametric form, and find out the value(s) of θθ.

Make equations of circles both start with x2+y2,x2+y2, subtract one from the other to get equation of its radical axis which is a straight line. Intersection of this radical axis and one of the circles can be found by plugging in for x or y of one circle into the other.

following the very nice hint of arkeet you can after defining a,b,c,g,h using rc1,zc1,rc2,zc2, as the center point of the circles and R1 and R2 their radii and assuming that rc2!=rc1 (the case where rc2=rc1 can be also done in the manner or arkeet, however the present assumption was useful to me)

g = (zc2 - zc1)/(rc2 - rc1); h = (R1*R1-R2*R2 + zc2*zc2 - zc1*zc1 + rc2*rc2 - rc1*rc1 )/(2*(rc2 - rc1));

a = (g*g+ 1); b = -2*g*h + 2*g*rc1 - 2*zc1; c = h*h -2*h*rc1 + rc1*rc1 + zc1*zc1 - R1*R1;

zplus = (-b +sqrt(b*b-4*a*c)) / (2*a); zminus = (-b -sqrt(b*b-4*a*c)) / (2*a); and rplus = -gzplus+h; rminus = -gzminus+h;

you can test this by verifying that the intersection points do lie on the 2 circles.
